Bulawayo · Heritage

Khami Ruins Historical Tour

A slow, story-rich day among terraced stone walls, river views and the echoes of a pre-colonial capital just outside Bulawayo.

Safari Journal · Field Notes

First light over Bulawayo

I'm pulling out my notebook as we roll through Bulawayo's wide avenues in the cool early light. Kombis and commuters are starting their day while we turn the vehicle west, leaving the city grid behind as thornveld and granite outcrops begin to take over the horizon.

As the road drops towards the Khami River, the air around us is already quieter. This isn't going to be a rush-through-the-site kind of excursion — I'm settling us into an unhurried walk through layers of stone, story and trade routes that once linked this plateau to the Indian Ocean.

Terraces, walls and viewpoints

We enter the Khami Ruins complex on foot, following low stone platforms towards the more dramatic terraced walls. Carved patterns appear in the masonry, and from the upper viewpoints we look down over the river valley fringed with reeds and fig trees.

I unpack how the city was organised — elite platforms, working spaces, ritual zones — and how Khami connected to a web of trade stretching to Mozambique and beyond. It feels both very close and very far from the asphalt of Bulawayo.

Guide's Highlight

Pause where the terrace edge is lowest and look back at the walls. It's the best angle to feel just how intentionally this landscape was shaped.

Midday by the river

By late morning we have descended towards the water, where birds move between reeds and overhanging branches. This is usually the quietest part of the site, perfect for a simple packed lunch and some time to sketch, photograph or just sit with the sound of the river.

For photographers

The angled afternoon light on the stonework can be spectacular. I suggest wide lenses for the terraces and a longer focal length for details in the patterned brickwork and for birdlife along the river corridor.

Afternoon return to Bulawayo

In the mid-afternoon we begin the easy drive back toward Bulawayo. Granite kopjes catch the last strong light, and I often suggest a quick stop for a final viewpoint over the surrounding countryside before we re-enter the city.

Back in Bulawayo by late afternoon, there is still time to add a museum visit or slow cafe stop before dinner — or simply to rest, knowing we've walked through one of the country's most important historic sites in a full, grounded way.

Reading the stonework

As the day unfolds you start to notice small differences in the walls themselves — courses that curve more tightly, pattern bands set into otherwise plain faces, and how certain terraces command views while others sit closer to sheltered spaces.

I like to pause at these details and invite you to imagine the labour, planning and symbolism involved. By the time we leave, the site has usually shifted from simply "old stones" to a legible, layered city in your mind.
