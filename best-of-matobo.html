Matabeleland South · Full Day

The Best of Matobo

A classic Matobo day that threads granite kopjes, San rock art and sweeping viewpoints into one slow, immersive journey.

Safari Journal · Field Notes

Morning among the kopjes

Right now we're leaving Bulawayo in the soft light of morning, watching the city thin out into small holdings and then into the granite country that defines Matobo. As I glance in the rear-view mirror I can see the first kopjes stacking up on the horizon, rounded boulders balanced in improbable ways.

A few minutes ago I turned us onto one of the quieter approaches into the park, where the road narrows and you start to feel wrapped in stone and sky. This is usually the moment our day begins to slow down — more viewpoints, more pauses, less hurry — and I can already feel everyone in the vehicle settling into that rhythm.

Rock art & stories

About an hour into the drive we are standing at one of my favourite morning stops — a rock art site. After a short walk we duck under an overhang to find ochre figures and animal forms painted on the rock. As you get your first glimpse I talk you through how these scenes connect to hunting, trance and the long history of the area's first people.

It is very quiet here right now, except for wind and birds. That mix of ancient marks and this morning's light is usually what my guests remember most strongly about Matobo.

Guide's Highlight

The best viewpoints often require just a few extra steps up a boulder. Take your time — the 360° view over kopjes and valleys is worth it.

Midday in the valleys

It's around midday now and we've dropped into one of the quieter valleys, where shade trees and balancing rocks make a natural picnic spot. Lunch is unhurried — simple, filling food and cold drinks, with time to just sit and take in the textures of lichen and stone in front of us.

For photographers

I always suggest guests bring both wide lenses for sweeping rock formations and tighter frames for tree silhouettes and lizard detail on sun-warmed granite.

Afternoon viewpoints & return

Mid-afternoon now, and I've looped us past one of the park's classic viewpoints. As I write this we can scan for raptors, feel the scale of the granite landscape and talk about why this area holds such a deep sense of place for many Zimbabweans.

A little later, on the drive back towards Bulawayo, conversation usually drops into a comfortable silence — golden light, long shadows and that steady sense of having spent a full, grounded day in Matobo together.

Logistics, seasons & wildlife

As I jot down these notes back in Bulawayo this evening, I'm already thinking about how different Matobo feels across the year. In the cool, dry winter months we often have crystal visibility and crisp mornings; in the build-up to the rains, clouds pile dramatically over the kopjes and afternoon storms arrive in short, theatrical bursts.

Today we spotted klipspringer, dassies and an impressive mix of raptors, with fresh signs of white rhino in one of the valleys. On most days I pace the route around both sightings and your energy, keeping driving distances short and walks comfortable so that the journal entry at the end of the day feels as unhurried as the hours themselves.
