Low-Impact Safaris with Small Operators

What "low-impact" really means on safari, and how Bushmen Travel puts that into practice around Bulawayo, Matobo and Victoria Falls.

8 min read Conservation · Trip Design

"Low-impact" has become a label that appears on everything from bottled water to long-haul flights. On safari, it can blur into marketing unless you can point to specific choices on the ground. For us at Bushmen Travel, the term only earns its place when it changes how we choose routes, partners, group sizes and daily rhythms.

This piece looks at what low-impact can mean in practice in Zimbabwe — especially around Bulawayo, the Matobo Hills and Victoria Falls — and how travelling with a smaller operator can make your trip gentler on landscapes and more generous to the communities that host you.

Four layers of impact

Carbon is a huge part of the conversation, but it's not the only lens. When we design or guide a Bushmen journey, we think about four overlapping layers:

  • Environmental: how many vehicles, how much driving, which trails and how close we get.
  • Community: who benefits from the fees you pay, where food is sourced, who tells the stories.
  • Cultural: how we handle sacred sites, photographs, music and oral histories.
  • Pace: whether the trip leaves you feeling scattered or grounded — which shapes what you share afterwards.

Low-impact safaris acknowledge all four. They use the same landscapes and wildlife as big-brand itineraries, but the way they move through that space feels different.

Small groups, slower days

One of the simplest levers is group size. A single vehicle with four guests behaves differently to a convoy of trucks. You can linger longer at a sighting without blocking others, choose quieter loops, and respond to animal behaviour rather than a timetable.

Our Matobo Rhino Encounter and Best of Matobo tours work best in these smaller configurations. Tracking white rhino on foot, reading spoor and listening to your guide unpack Bushmen paintings feels very different when you're one of a handful, not one of twenty.

Choosing local partners and routes

In and around Bulawayo and Victoria Falls we work with guides, rangers and lodge teams who live with these landscapes year-round. They notice when a track is becoming overused, when a waterhole needs a rest from vehicle pressure, or when a community event is happening near a village we pass through.

That local knowledge feeds back into routing: which side roads we take to Khami Ruins, how we sequence walks and drives in Matobo, when we suggest spending an extra night in Bulawayo to support small guesthouses and township eateries rather than sprinting straight to the next park.

Staying longer, moving less

Another lever is time. A week in one region is almost always lighter than a week spent hopping between four. We encourage guests to choose fewer bases and go deeper: a Bulawayo and Matobo arc, or a circuit that links Matobo, Khami and Victoria Falls rather than scattering nights across the map.

That approach dovetails with carbon-aware flying too. If you're already making the effort of a long-haul trip to Zimbabwe, shaping a longer, slower stay is often the most meaningful climate decision available to an individual traveller.

What you can ask any operator

Whether you travel with us or someone else, a few simple questions can reveal a lot about how seriously a safari company takes impact:

  • How big are your typical groups, and can you run this privately or as a small party?
  • Which communities benefit financially from this itinerary?
  • How do your guides decide when to leave a sighting or give an animal space?
  • How often do you revisit your routes to ease pressure on particular tracks or viewpoints?

Clear, specific answers matter more than slogans. If an operator can explain trade-offs honestly, you're more likely to be part of a trip that feels aligned with your values.

How Bushmen builds low-impact days

In our own planning, low-impact often means:

  • Keeping vehicle numbers modest on Matobo rhino walks and game drives.
  • Pairing headline sites like Victoria Falls with township food stops, markets and community craft hubs.
  • Choosing accommodations that employ locally and support nearby schools or conservation projects.
  • Building in unscheduled time so you're not chasing a checklist from dawn to dark.

None of this makes a trip hair-shirt or joyless. The goal is still wonder, humour and good stories — just held in a way that feels fair to the people and places that make them possible.
