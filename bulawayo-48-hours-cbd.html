48 Hours in the Bulawayo CBD

A two-day walk through Bulawayo's grid of wide avenues, old arcades, and leafy side streets — designed for travellers who want to understand the City of Kings at street level.

7 min read CBD · Avenues

Bulawayo's city centre invites walking. Its generous pavements, mature trees, and human-scale buildings make it easy to slow down. This 48-hour loop keeps you mostly on foot, weaving between cafes, galleries, and small parks while leaving space for serendipity.

Day One · Avenues & Arcades

Morning · Fourth Avenue & Main Street

Start early along Fourth Avenue, when traffic is still thin and the light is soft. Look up: verandas, colonnades, and painted signage tell the story of a commercial town that grew along rail lines.

Settle into a corner cafe for a slow breakfast — eggs, toast, and strong coffee. Watch the morning choreography: school uniforms, delivery trucks, and long-haul buses arriving from Harare and Victoria Falls.

Afternoon · Galleries & City Hall lawns

Drift towards local galleries and craft spaces dotted around the CBD. Many are small, with handwritten opening hours and experimental shows that blend painting, photography, and sculpture.

Later, pause by the City Hall lawns. Sit on a bench, feel the scale of the avenues and how the city opens up to the sky. This is where you sense Bulawayo's slower pace compared to Harare.

Day Two · Railways & Rooftops

Morning · National Railways Museum

Head towards the National Railways Museum, where old steam engines and carriages sit in open air. Walk slowly between locomotives, reading plaques and tracing your hand along worn steel and brass.

It's an outdoor archive of movement: the network that once linked Bulawayo to ports and mines across the region. Give yourself time to imagine the journeys that began here.

Blue Hour · A rooftop drink

End your 48 hours above street level. Find a rooftop bar or hotel terrace with a view over the CBD grid. Order something citrusy and watch the city change colour as the sun drops behind distant kopjes.
